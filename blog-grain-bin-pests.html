Preventing Insect Infestation in Nebraska Grain Bins

Agricultural · By the Chem-Tech Team · Fall 2025 · 7 min read

Nebraska farmers work hard to bring in a quality harvest. But once the grain goes into the bin, a new threat emerges: stored-grain insects. Infestation can render grain unmarketable, trigger mold growth, and generate heat that degrades quality far faster than most farmers expect. Protecting stored grain from insects is as important as the harvest itself.

Why Stored Grain Is Vulnerable

Grain bins create ideal conditions for pest populations: consistent temperature (early in storage), abundant food, and limited airflow in non-aerated bins. Many grain insects are already present at low levels at harvest — hitchhiking in on the grain itself or arriving from carryover residue left in the bin from prior years.

The problem compounds quickly. Stored grain temperatures above 60°F allow most grain insects to breed continuously. A single bin can go from a manageable low-level infestation to a full-scale loss in one warm Nebraska fall.

Nebraska's Most Common Stored-Grain Insects

Maize Weevil & Granary Weevil

The most destructive primary pests of stored corn and small grains. The female bores into individual kernels to lay eggs, and larvae develop inside — invisible until the adult chews its way out. A heavily infested bin will appear normal until the grain is probed or moved.

Indianmeal Moth

The most common grain-storage moth in the U.S. Larvae spin silken webbing through the grain surface, creating clumping and hot spots. Adults are the first sign most farmers notice — small reddish-brown moths flying near the bin at dusk.

Red Flour Beetle & Confused Flour Beetle

Secondary pests that feed on broken kernels, fines, and grain dust. They thrive in bins that aren't cleaned between seasons and move freely through the grain mass.

Lesser Grain Borer

A primary pest capable of boring into whole corn kernels. Creates large amounts of fine powder (frass) that can clog aeration systems and contribute to mycotoxin problems.

Know the Risk

Infested grain can be rejected at the elevator or heavily discounted. Beyond economic loss, some pest-damaged grain produces mycotoxins that are unsafe for livestock feed.

Pre-Harvest: The Most Important Window

The best time to prevent a grain bin infestation is before the grain goes in. Follow this pre-harvest checklist every year:

  1. Empty and clean the bin completely. Remove all old grain, fines, and dust. Insects overwinter in carryover grain and debris. A shop vac and pressure washer reach areas a broom misses.
  2. Inspect aeration floors and ducts. Broken or rusted perforated floors trap fines and create insect harborage. Repair or replace before filling.
  3. Apply a bin-floor insecticide. A licensed applicator can treat bin walls, floors, and auger systems with approved grain-protectant residuals before filling.
  4. Check and seal the roof and sidewalls. Bird and rodent entry points introduce insects and moisture. Seal gaps and repair holes.

During Storage: Monitor and Control Temperature

Once grain is in the bin, consistent monitoring is your best defense:

  • Aerate to cool the grain mass. Target 35–40°F for long-term winter storage in Nebraska. Most grain insects cannot reproduce below 50°F and die at sustained temperatures below 20°F.
  • Probe the grain regularly. Insert a grain probe at multiple depths and locations. Look for elevated moisture, clumping, off odors, and visible insects or webbing.
  • Check for hot spots. Insect activity generates heat. A temperature cable system or monthly probing with a handheld thermometer can detect developing hot spots before they spread.
  • Turn the grain. Running the auger to move grain from lower to upper levels exposes insects to cool air and breaks up clumping.

When Fumigation Is Necessary

When insect populations are already established, fumigation with phosphine (aluminum phosphide) is the only treatment that reaches insects throughout the grain mass — including those inside kernels where contact insecticides can't penetrate.

Phosphine fumigation is a licensed, regulated process. It requires:

  • A licensed applicator (required by Nebraska state law)
  • Complete bin sealing to maintain lethal concentrations for the full exposure period
  • Aeration to purge the bin before re-entry
  • Proper documentation for grain that will enter the food or feed supply
