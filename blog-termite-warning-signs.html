7 Warning Signs of Termite Damage in Your Home

Termites · By the Chem-Tech Team · Spring 2025 · 6 min read

Termites cause an estimated $5 billion in property damage every year in the United States — and most of it happens before homeowners ever notice a problem. In Nebraska, subterranean termites are the primary threat, attacking homes from the ground up and hollowing out structural wood while the surface looks completely normal.

The good news: termite damage is rarely a surprise for those who know what to look for. Here are the 7 warning signs every Nebraska homeowner should recognize.

Sign 1: Mud Tubes on Exterior Walls or Foundation

Subterranean termites build pencil-width mud tubes to travel between their underground colony and the wood they feed on. These tubes protect them from the open air and from predators. You'll most commonly find them running up exterior foundation walls, along basement walls, inside crawl spaces, or behind water heaters and HVAC units.

If you find a mud tube, break a small section out of the middle. If termites are active, worker termites will be visible inside or the tube will be repaired within a day or two.

Don't Ignore This

A mud tube — even an old, inactive one — means termites have accessed your home before. An inspection is essential to determine whether the colony is still active.

Sign 2: Hollow-Sounding Wood

Termites eat wood from the inside out, following the grain and leaving a thin outer shell. Tap suspect wood with a screwdriver handle — a dull, hollow sound instead of a solid thud indicates termite damage inside. Check baseboards, door frames, window sills, wood paneling, and structural beams in basements and crawl spaces.

Sign 3: Spongy or Sagging Floors

When subfloor joists or support beams are extensively damaged, floors begin to feel soft, spongy, or bouncy underfoot. In severe cases, you may notice actual sagging or dipping in floor sections. This is a sign of significant structural damage and requires immediate professional attention.

Sign 4: Frass (Termite Droppings)

Drywood termites — less common in Nebraska than subterranean types — leave behind frass: tiny pellets of digested wood that look like coarse sawdust or coffee grounds. You'll find frass piles below kick-out holes in wood surfaces. Subterranean termites incorporate their droppings into their mud tubes, so frass is less visible with them.

Chem-Tech Tip

Frass can look similar to sawdust from carpenter ants or other wood-boring insects. A professional inspection is the only reliable way to confirm the species and extent of damage.

Sign 5: Discarded Wings Near Windows and Doors

In spring, reproductive termites (called swarmers or alates) leave their colony to start new ones. After landing, they shed their wings — leaving small, equal-length wing pairs near windows, doors, vents, and light sources. Finding discarded wings indoors is a strong sign of an active colony nearby. Swarmer termites are often mistaken for flying ants, but termite wings are equal in length while ant wings are not.

Sign 6: Tight-Fitting Doors and Windows

As termites consume wood, the structural changes can cause door frames and window frames to warp or shift. Doors and windows that suddenly stick or don't close properly — especially in the absence of recent moisture or weather changes — can indicate hidden termite activity in the surrounding framing.

Sign 7: Bubbling or Uneven Paint

Paint that bubbles, peels, or looks uneven without obvious water damage may be hiding termite activity below. As termites tunnel close to the surface of walls, they create moisture pockets and undermine the paint's adhesion. This sign is commonly overlooked because it resembles ordinary paint failure.

What to Do If You See These Signs

Do not attempt DIY termite treatment. By the time visible signs appear, the colony has typically been active for 3–5 years. Over-the-counter products cannot reach the colony itself and will only drive termites deeper into the structure.
