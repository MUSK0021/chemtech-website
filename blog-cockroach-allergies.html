Spring Allergy Season: Could Cockroaches Be the Cause?

Tips · By the Chem-Tech Team · Spring 2025 · 5 min read

Every spring, millions of Americans reach for antihistamines and blame pollen for their sneezing, itchy eyes, and runny noses. But if your allergy symptoms seem worse inside than outside — or persist well beyond pollen season — cockroaches may be the hidden culprit lurking in your Nebraska home.

How Cockroaches Trigger Allergies

Cockroach allergens come from several sources: their saliva, shed skins (called exuvia), egg casings, and droppings. These particles become airborne and are inhaled — triggering the same immune responses as dust mites or pet dander.

According to the National Pest Management Association (NPMA), cockroach allergens affect an estimated 26 million Americans with asthma and are a leading cause of childhood asthma attacks in urban and suburban areas. A study by the National Institute of Environmental Health Sciences found cockroach allergens present in 63% of U.S. homes — including suburban and rural properties.

Important

Cockroach allergens don't disappear when the infestation is treated — they can persist in carpets, upholstery, and walls for months. Professional remediation combined with thorough cleaning is essential.

Symptoms That Point to Cockroach Allergy

Cockroach allergy symptoms closely mimic other common allergies, which makes them easy to misattribute. Watch for:

  • Sneezing and runny or stuffy nose that doesn't follow outdoor pollen patterns
  • Itchy, red, or watery eyes — especially indoors or in certain rooms
  • Skin rashes or eczema flare-ups
  • Coughing, wheezing, or shortness of breath
  • Symptoms that worsen at night (cockroaches are most active after dark)
  • Children who seem to have chronic cold-like symptoms indoors

If your symptoms are consistently worse in the kitchen, bathroom, or basement, cockroach allergens are a strong suspect. These are the rooms where cockroach populations concentrate.

Nebraska's Cockroach Problem

Eastern Nebraska's warm, humid summers create ideal conditions for cockroach activity. The German cockroach — the most common indoor species — thrives in kitchens and bathrooms. The American cockroach favors basements, utility rooms, and crawl spaces. Both are found across the Omaha metro, Fremont, and surrounding communities.

Cockroaches are elusive. A home can have a significant population before a single roach is spotted in daylight. By then, allergen levels in the home are already elevated.

Chem-Tech Tip

Seeing one cockroach during the day is a red flag — it typically means the nighttime population is large enough to push individuals out during daylight hours. Call us immediately.

7 Prevention Steps You Can Take Today

  1. Seal entry points. Caulk gaps around pipes under sinks, behind appliances, and where utilities enter the home.
  2. Fix moisture issues. Cockroaches are attracted to water. Repair leaky pipes, use a bathroom exhaust fan, and keep crawl spaces dry.
  3. Store food properly. Use sealed containers for dry goods. Never leave food out overnight.
  4. Clean behind appliances. Grease and crumbs behind refrigerators and stoves are prime cockroach food sources.
  5. Empty trash regularly. Use lidded trash cans and take bags out frequently.
  6. Vacuum often. Regular vacuuming removes allergens and egg casings before populations grow.
  7. Schedule an inspection. Professional monitoring catches early infestations before allergen levels rise.

When to Call a Professional

Over-the-counter sprays and roach baits offer short-term relief but rarely eliminate an established infestation. German cockroaches in particular breed rapidly — a single female and her offspring can theoretically produce 30,000 cockroaches in a year. At-home treatments push populations into walls and voids, making them harder to reach.
