Bed Bugs: How to Spot Them Before They Spread

Bed Bugs · By the Chem-Tech Team · Summer 2025 · 6 min read

Bed bugs have made a major resurgence across the United States over the past two decades — including throughout Nebraska. They travel easily in luggage, clothing, secondhand furniture, and even library books, making virtually any home vulnerable regardless of cleanliness. The difference between a manageable early treatment and a whole-home infestation is almost entirely about how quickly you catch them.

Understanding Bed Bug Behavior

Bed bugs (Cimex lectularius) are nocturnal, flat, reddish-brown insects about the size of an apple seed when fully grown. They feed exclusively on blood — preferring humans — and typically feed once every 5–10 days. During the day they hide in tight, dark spaces within a few feet of where you sleep: mattress seams, box spring folds, headboard crevices, and behind electrical outlet covers.

A single fertilized female can lay 1–5 eggs per day and up to 500 over her lifetime. At room temperature, eggs hatch in about 10 days and nymphs reach reproductive maturity in 5–6 weeks. Early detection is everything.

The 6 Signs of a Bed Bug Infestation

1. Rust-Colored Stains on Bedding

When bed bugs feed, they can be accidentally crushed — leaving small rust or reddish-brown blood stains on sheets, pillowcases, or mattresses. You may also see very small dark spots (dried excrement) about the size of a ballpoint pen tip. These spots often bleed into fabric like a marker would.

2. Shed Skins (Exoskeletons)

Bed bugs molt five times as they mature, leaving behind pale, translucent shed skins. Finding multiple shed skins — especially concentrated in mattress seams, box spring folds, or behind the headboard — is a reliable sign of an established population. A single sighting of a shed skin warrants a full inspection.

3. Live Bed Bugs

Adult bed bugs are visible to the naked eye — flat, oval, and reddish-brown. After feeding they become more elongated and darker. Nymphs (juveniles) are smaller and lighter in color, making them harder to spot. Use a flashlight and credit card to probe mattress seams, box spring staples, and headboard joints during a daytime inspection.

4. A Sweet, Musty Odor

Large infestations produce a distinctive sweet, musty odor often described as overripe raspberries or almonds. This smell comes from the bugs' scent glands and is typically only noticeable with a significant population. If you notice this odor in a bedroom without another explanation, call a professional immediately.

5. Bite Marks

Bed bug bites appear as small, red, itchy welts — often in a line or cluster of 3 ("breakfast, lunch, and dinner" pattern). They are commonly found on exposed skin while sleeping: neck, arms, shoulders, and hands. However, about 30% of people do not react to bed bug bites at all, meaning bites alone are unreliable for confirming an infestation. Never rely solely on bites as your indicator.

6. Small Dark Spots on Walls or Furniture

Bed bugs leave fecal spotting not just on bedding but along their travel paths — on baseboards, behind picture frames, along outlet covers, and in furniture joints. Finding a trail of dark spots from the bed area toward a wall is a strong indicator of active movement and an established population.

Don't Make This Mistake

Throwing away your mattress will not solve a bed bug problem. Bugs live throughout the room — in baseboards, furniture, and walls. Discarding infested furniture can also spread bugs to hallways and neighbors if not properly sealed first.

Where to Inspect First

Conduct your inspection systematically, working outward from the sleeping area:

  1. Mattress seams, tufts, and tags
  2. Box spring — remove the dust cover stapled to the bottom
  3. Headboard and footboard joints and crevices
  4. Bedside furniture (nightstands, dressers) — especially drawer tracks and joints
  5. Baseboards and carpet edges near the bed
  6. Behind electrical outlet and switch plate covers
  7. Behind hanging wall art and picture frames
After Travel

After any hotel stay, inspect your luggage outside before bringing it in. Wash all clothing on high heat immediately. Store luggage in sealed plastic bags or in a heated garage — never in a bedroom closet.

Why Professional Treatment Is Essential

Heat treatment and professional chemical application are the only reliably effective methods for eliminating bed bugs. Over-the-counter sprays kill bugs they contact but do not penetrate harborage sites where the population lives. Bugs quickly develop resistance to common pyrethroids found in retail products.
